# prep-presentation — family playbook

A presentation is judged on **three independent axes**, and weakness on any one tanks the
whole thing: **Content** (is the story right?), **Delivery** (do they believe the person?),
**Logistics** (does the room/tech cooperate?). Prep all three. Most people over-prep content
and under-prep delivery and logistics — that asymmetry is where talks fail.

## Coverage map (MECE categories for any talk)
Score each cell Probability×Impact×(5−Readiness); work top-priority first.
1. **Content / Story** — the narrative arc, the one core message, slide-by-slide spine.
2. **Delivery / Presence** — voice, pacing, pauses, eye contact, body, opening hook, close.
3. **Logistics** — room/Teams/hybrid setup, screen-share, demo playback, clicker, timing.
4. **Visual design** — one idea per slide, big visuals, minimal text, readable from the back.
5. **Q&A defense** — anticipated questions, hostile follow-ups, "I don't know" recovery.
6. **Audience-fit** — altitude calibration for a mixed/multi-level room; what each segment wants.
7. **Conversion/outcome** — the implicit or explicit ask; what you want the room to DO next.

## The core message rule
Every talk reduces to **ONE sentence** the audience repeats afterward. Write it first; if a
slide doesn't serve it, cut the slide. (Duarte's "big idea"; Heath "find the core".)

## Narrative spine (use one)
- **SCQA / Minto** (exec default): Situation → Complication → Question → Answer, answer-first.
- **What/So-what/Now-what** or **Problem → Insight → Solution → Proof → Impact → Vision → Ask.**
- **TED "throughline"**: one idea, built one connected step at a time.
- **Hero's journey (light)** for personal/project arc: the struggle is what makes it land.
Open with the conclusion (BLUF) — execs decide whether to keep listening in ~60 seconds.

## Slide budget (rule of thumb)
~1–1.5 min per slide. 20-min talk ≈ 11–14 content slides. One idea per slide. ≤6 lines /
≤6 words per line, or a single image/diagram. Never read slides verbatim — slides support
the spoken story, they are not the script. (Reynolds *Presentation Zen*; Kawasaki 10/20/30.)

## "Present like a CEO" — executive-presence checklist
- **Altitude first.** Lead with why it matters to the business/mission, not the mechanics.
  Go low only when someone reaches for it. Not drowning a technical room in detail is itself
  impressive.
- **BLUF.** Bottom line up front, every time — talk and each Q&A answer.
- **Slow down. Pause.** Silence after a key line signals confidence; rushing signals nerves.
  Aim for ~130 wpm, deliberate. Resist filling pauses.
- **Vocal authority.** Down-inflect at sentence ends (statements, not questions). Vary pace
  and volume. Cut filler ("um", "like", "kind of", "I think maybe").
- **Own it.** "I decided / I built / I got this wrong and changed it." Ownership > "we were
  told to." Credit collaborators generously — confident people share credit.
- **Data-backed + honest.** Quantify impact; never invent a number. Naming a gap honestly
  beats a fabricated metric — leaders are listening for over-claiming.
- **Vision close.** End on where this goes, not "that's all, thanks." Leaders buy trajectory.
- **Command, don't survive, Q&A.** Treat questions as the best part. Repeat/reframe the
  question, answer the bottom line, then support. "Great question" sparingly.
- **Stance & stillness.** Plant feet, open posture, hands visible, purposeful gestures. Power
  pose privately beforehand (Cuddy). Smile on the open.

## Hybrid / remote tactics (when part of the room is on Teams)
- Look at the **camera** for the open, the close, and any direct address — that's eye contact
  for remote viewers; sweep the physical room in between.
- Assign a **co-pilot** in the room to watch Teams chat/raised hands so remote isn't second-class.
- **Screen-share dry-run** on the actual setup: share the slides app (not whole screen),
  confirm remote can see the **demo video with audio** (share sound!).
- Have the deck **open and pre-loaded**; close notifications/Slack/Teams popups; charge +
  plug in; wired network if possible. Have a **PDF backup** and the deck in the cloud.

## Demo rule (de-risking)
If a live demo can fail, **pre-record it as primary** and *offer* live in Q&A ("happy to run
it live if you'd like"). A pre-recorded demo is tight, narratable, and immune to network/stage
fright. Always narrate over a demo — never play it silently.

## Question-space taxonomy (build the Q&A bank from these)
- **Why does this matter? / what breaks without it?** (business value)
- **How do you know it works?** (validation, rigor — be honest about what's not done)
- **What was hardest? / a decision you made?** (ownership, judgment)
- **How does it generalize / scale?** (platform vs one-off)
- **What's the risk / what could go wrong?** (maturity of thinking)
- **What's novel here vs off-the-shelf?** (differentiation / IP)
- **What would you do differently?** (growth mindset — pick ONE real thing)
- **What's next / what do you need?** (vision + the ask; often a conversion question in disguise)
- **Curveball / hostile reframe** for every claim: "isn't this over-engineered?" "couldn't a
  tool do this?" — pre-write the calm one-liner.

## Pre-mortem seeds (the classic presentation failure modes)
Ran over time · info-dumped and lost the story · read slides · monotone/no pauses · demo
failed · over-claimed a number and got caught · wrong altitude for a mixed room · flat ending
with no vision/ask · hybrid tech failed · froze on a hostile question · nerves spiked tempo.

## Rehearsal protocol (active, spaced, graduated)
- **Active only**: rehearse out loud, standing, timed. Reading the deck silently ≠ rehearsal.
- **Spaced** over the runway, not crammed the night before.
- **Graduated stress**: solo → record yourself & watch → rehearse to 1–2 people → full dress
  rehearsal on the real setup. Final rehearsal is the highest pressure.
- **Time every run.** Build for ~90% of the slot; talks expand live under nerves.
- **Memorize only the open (first ~30s) and the close.** Improvise the middle off the spine —
  memorized middles sound robotic and shatter if interrupted.
- **24-hour cutoff**: no new slides/content in the final day. Consolidate, logistics, rest.

## Curated delivery resources (recommend per need)
- **Story/structure**: Nancy Duarte *Resonate* / *slide:ology* + her TED "secret structure of
  great talks"; Chris Anderson *TED Talks: The Official Guide*; Barbara Minto *The Pyramid
  Principle* (exec logic); Carmine Gallo *Talk Like TED* & *The Presentation Secrets of Steve Jobs*.
- **Slide design**: Garr Reynolds *Presentation Zen*; Cole Nussbaumer Knaflic *Storytelling
  with Data*; Guy Kawasaki 10/20/30 rule.
- **Voice/delivery**: Julian Treasure TED "How to speak so that people want to listen"; Vinh
  Giang (accent/voice/stagecraft); Patsy Rodenburg on presence.
- **Presence/psychology**: Amy Cuddy *Presence*; Sylvia Ann Hewlett *Executive Presence*;
  Alison Wood Brooks "get excited" (reframe nerves as readiness).
- **Exec narrative**: Amazon/Bezos 6-pager + working-backwards thinking (clarity over flash).

## Anti-patterns
Wall-of-text slides · reading verbatim · no core message · mechanics before motivation ·
flat ending · silent demo · cramming · memorizing the whole script · ignoring the remote half ·
inventing metrics · surviving Q&A instead of commanding it.
